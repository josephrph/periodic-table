What do the boxes shown represent?+

Each of the 65 boxes represents either a molecule of the cannabis plant or a precursor acid. There are 56 molecules (cannabinoids, terpenes, flavonoids, and rare & novel cannabinoids) and 9 precursor acids shown.

A molecule is a group of atoms bonded together—the smallest unit of a chemical compound that retains its chemical properties. A precursor acid is a compound that converts into its active non-acidic form under certain conditions (e.g., THCA becomes THC when heated).

How were the symbols determined?+

Symbols are two-to-five letter shorthand notations based on English names, Latin names, or discoverer names. They are publicly used in the scientific literature and help scientists communicate efficiently about each molecule.

Why is the chart organized into five sections?+

The five sections reflect the common scientific categories of cannabis plant molecules: Phytocannabinoids, Terpenes, Flavonoids, Precursor Acids, and (new in V2) Rare & Novel Cannabinoids. Molecules with increasing molecular weight are ordered left to right in rows (periods), similar to the traditional periodic table of elements.

What does each section represent?+

Cannabinoids interact with the body’s endocannabinoid system to regulate pain, mood, appetite, and immune function. Over 100 have been identified in cannabis.

Terpenes are aromatic organic compounds produced in cannabis trichomes alongside cannabinoids. Common examples include myrcene, limonene, and caryophyllene, and they are believed to offer therapeutic benefits.

Flavonoids are polyphenol plant compounds that may work synergistically with cannabinoids and terpenes to enhance medicinal properties—the “Entourage Effect.”

Precursor Acids are biosynthetic forms of cannabinoids that convert to their active counterparts upon decarboxylation (heat exposure).

Rare & Novel Cannabinoids (V2 new) are low-abundance but pharmacologically significant compounds such as THCP, CBDP, and CBND.

How were the atomic masses calculated?+

Atomic masses were determined from public references or by summing each element’s molecular mass from the chemical formula. The table is then arranged so that molecules with increasing mass are ordered from left to right in each row.

What does the evidence rating (A–D) mean?+

Grade A — Strong, replicated human clinical evidence.

Grade B — Promising human evidence, limited in scale; positive preclinical data.

Grade C — Primarily preclinical (animal or in vitro) evidence.

Grade D — Early-stage or theoretical evidence only.

What do the boxes shown represent?+

Each of the 65 boxes on the Cannabis Periodic Table of Molecules™ chart represents either a molecule of the cannabis plant or a precursor acid. There are 56 molecules (cannabinoids, terpenes, flavonoids, and rare & novel cannabinoids) and 9 precursor acids shown. A molecule and a precursor acid are two different chemical entities with distinct characteristics and functions.

A molecule is a group of atoms bonded together. It is the smallest unit of a chemical compound that retains the chemical properties of that compound. Molecules can be simple, containing only a few atoms, or complex, consisting of hundreds or thousands of atoms.

A precursor acid refers to a chemical compound that participates in a chemical reaction producing other compounds. Under certain conditions it can convert the molecule into its non-acidic form. Precursor acids typically contain one or more acidic functional groups, such as a carboxyl group (–COOH).

For example, THCa is the acidic form of THC. Although THCa does have medicinal properties, it does not have psychoactive properties. The process of decarboxylation requires exposure to heat. When vaporizing or smoking, high temperatures quickly decarboxylate THCa to THC, enabling the body to absorb it through the lungs. Oxygen exposure can also contribute to the decarboxylation process.

Why is the chart organized into five sections?+

The organization of the Cannabis Periodic Table of Molecules™ chart was based on the common scientific assessment of the cannabis plant as a whole. Cannabinoids (phytocannabinoids and rare & novel variants), terpenes, flavonoids, and precursor acids are commonly recognized categories. What does each section represent?+

A cannabinoid is a type of chemical compound found in the cannabis plant, as well as in some other plants and in the human body. Cannabinoids interact with the body’s endocannabinoid system, which is involved in regulating a wide range of physiological processes such as pain, mood, appetite, and immune function. There are over 100 cannabinoids identified in the cannabis plant, including THC and CBD, which are the most well-known.

Terpenes are a large and diverse class of organic compounds found in many plants, including cannabis. Produced in the same glandular trichomes as cannabinoids, they contribute to aroma and flavor and are believed to have a range of potential therapeutic benefits. Common examples include myrcene, limonene, pinene, and caryophyllene.

Flavonoids are plant compounds belonging to the larger class of polyphenols, widely distributed in fruits, vegetables, and medicinal plants including cannabis. Several flavonoids retain medicinal value that may work in concert with cannabinoids and terpenes to enhance the medical properties of the cannabis plant—commonly known as the “Entourage Effect.”

Precursor Acids are the biosynthetic forms of cannabinoids, produced by the plant before decarboxylation converts them into their active counterparts (e.g., THCA becomes THC when heated).

Rare & Novel Cannabinoids (new in Version 2) represent a growing category of less-abundant but scientifically significant cannabis compounds such as THCP, CBDP, CBND, and others, where emerging research suggests unique pharmacological activity beyond classic cannabinoids.

How were the atomic masses calculated?+

The atomic masses shown in the Cannabis Periodic Table of Molecules™ chart were determined by public references or the chemical formula. In the case of the latter, each element’s molecular mass was identified and totaled. For example, for water (H&sub2;O), two hydrogen atoms with mass ≈1 each and one oxygen atom with mass ≈16 total 18 for the molecule. After identifying or calculating the atomic masses of all the molecules, the table was constructed to reflect an objective structure, similar to the traditional periodic table of elements.

What does the evidence rating shown on each molecule tile mean?+

Each molecule tile in Version 2 displays a letter-grade evidence rating (A, B, C, or D) in the upper-right corner, reflecting the strength of published clinical and preclinical research supporting the molecule’s therapeutic applications:

Grade A — Strong, replicated human clinical evidence with broad scientific consensus.

Grade B — Promising human evidence, though limited in scale or scope; positive preclinical data supports further investigation.

Grade C — Primarily preclinical (animal or in vitro) evidence with limited human studies to date.

Grade D — Early-stage or theoretical evidence only; molecule is of emerging scientific interest.

Evidence ratings are assigned by Acannability based on review of the published literature and are updated as new research becomes available.

What receptor systems are shown on each molecule tile?+

Version 2 identifies which of five major receptor systems each molecule is known to engage. These receptor interactions are central to understanding how each compound produces its physiological effects:

CB1 (Cannabinoid Receptor 1) — Present primarily in the central nervous system. CB1 activation is responsible for the psychoactive effects of THC, as well as analgesia, appetite stimulation, and antiemetic properties. 16 molecules in the table engage CB1.

CB2 (Cannabinoid Receptor 2) — Found predominantly in immune tissues and peripheral organs. CB2 activation is associated with anti-inflammatory, immunomodulatory, and neuroprotective effects, typically without psychoactivity. 23 molecules engage CB2.

TRP (Transient Receptor Potential Channels) — Including TRPV1 and TRPA1, these ion channels are involved in pain signaling, temperature sensation, and inflammation. Several terpenes and cannabinoids modulate TRP channels to produce analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects. 19 molecules engage TRP channels.

PPAR (Peroxisome Proliferator-Activated Receptors) — Nuclear receptors involved in metabolism, inflammation regulation, and cell growth. PPAR activation by certain cannabinoids and flavonoids has been associated with anti-tumor, anti-diabetic, and cardiovascular effects. 19 molecules engage PPAR receptors.

5HT (Serotonin Receptors) — Particularly 5HT1A, serotonin receptor modulation is associated with anxiolytic, antidepressant, and antipsychotic effects. CBD is among the most studied molecules for 5HT activity. 13 molecules engage serotonin receptors.

Receptor data is based on published pharmacological literature. A single molecule may engage multiple receptor systems simultaneously.

What is the ‘Rare & Novel’ cannabinoid category?+

The Rare & Novel category was introduced in Version 2 to recognize a growing body of scientific literature on cannabis-derived cannabinoids that are present in very small concentrations in the plant but exhibit unique and potentially significant pharmacological properties.

Examples include Δ9-Tetrahydrocannabiphorol (THCP), which has demonstrated a binding affinity for CB1 receptors significantly higher than THC, and Cannabidiphorol (CBDP), the propyl-chain analog of CBD. Others include Cannabinodiol (CBND), Cannabicyclol (CBL), Cannabielsoin (CBE), and Cannabicyclolic Acid (CBLA).

As cannabis science matures, Acannability expects this category to grow as additional rare compounds are characterized and their clinical relevance is established.

What is the Entourage Effect and how is it displayed in this table?+

The Entourage Effect is a widely discussed concept in cannabis pharmacology describing how the many molecules of the cannabis plant—cannabinoids, terpenes, and flavonoids—may work together synergistically to produce effects that are greater or different than any single isolated compound. The idea was popularized by Dr. Raphael Mechoulam and has influenced how patients, clinicians, and product developers think about whole-plant versus isolate therapies.

In Version 2, the Entourage Effect Mapping tool (accessible from the header toolbar) allows users to select one or more molecules and visualize known molecular interactions and synergies highlighted across the table. This feature is intended for educational exploration and is based on published scientific literature reviewed by Acannability.
